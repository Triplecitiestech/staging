## 01 · The Core Model

The offering is **two distinct, separately-sold motions.** Keeping them separate is the single most important rule in this playbook — conflate them and the wrong client thinks they can call us to "snap our fingers" on custom work for a flat monthly fee.

**AI Enablement Services — the recurring practice.**
We get a business set up to use AI well and keep it running: setup on Claude (account, domain, AI Acceptable Use Policy), up to three pre-built integrations, governance, user enablement, and light ongoing management. It rides on top of the IT foundation we already manage, so the margin is strong. It is active, not "set and forget," because the AI landscape moves fast.

**AI Implementation — the project layer.**
The one-offs that ride on top: custom GPTs, custom skills, Cowork automations, workflows, and connectors beyond the included three. Each is scoped, quoted, and billed on its own, and each can carry its own monthly maintenance fee once delivered. In the near term, these projects out-earn the recurring fee.

**We are not a development house.** We assemble and configure AI on existing platforms; we do not sell ground-up application development. There is a massive gap between an app that works for one person and one that scales to hundreds, and a couple of custom builds under support would swamp us. We'll still take the occasional larger build for an existing client (e.g., replacing one legacy app) — but as a one-off with its own build fee *and* a maintenance fee, never as a marketed service line.

**The sequencing rule:** you can't sell the project layer until the enablement foundation is in place. Enablement first, every time. The natural path: a client adopts enablement, gets a taste of what AI can do, then buys an implementation project to push past the out-of-the-box limits.

## 02 · Go-to-Market — Lead With the Problem

**Take the client's AI temperature first.** Before anything else, find out where they stand:
- **Resistant / not interested** → plant the seed and move on: "When you're ready to look at AI, involve us — whether that's with us or another vendor." No push.
- **Curious / hesitant / "we keep hearing about it"** → this is most people. Lead into the assessment.
- **Already using it** (paying for ChatGPT, knee-deep with another vendor) → we don't say no. We help them get more out of what they already have.

**Lead with the business problem, not the technology.** We uncover the problems a business is having; the ones AI can solve, we bring up. We never open with "buy our AI package." We open with "we can help your business do *this*," and the readiness work is simply *how we get there responsibly.*

**The framing that always lands.** Position AI the way you'd explain a foundational shift:

> "AI right now is like the first time personal computers landed in a business, or the first time the internet really got used. It's that broad — and we're in the AOL era, so much is still coming. All of these things solve real business problems. As your trusted IT advisor, we'll help you figure out which ones actually move your business. Let me show you what it's already doing for other companies. Does that sound like something that might help you?"

No one fails to understand that approach — especially owners who remember how painful computers and the early internet felt at first.

**Justify it with the client's own numbers.** Once they're engaged, we put a dollar figure on the opportunity using *their* figures. Example: average deal $1,000, currently closing 20%; lift it to 30% (a 50% increase). On 50 deals that's roughly $50k in new revenue; if the AI tool cost ~$5k up front and ~$500/month, they net ~$40k. If they don't know their numbers, that's another gap our assessment exposes.

## 03 · The AI Profit & Readiness Assessment — the Paid Front Door

**This is the product.** Everyone interested starts here. There is no shrink-wrapped product to sell; the assessment is the engagement, and everything downstream is scoped from it.

**What the client buys:** an honest answer to *"Should we use AI, can we, and in what order?"* — delivered as one report. A real analysis, not a pitch.

**Price:**
- **$1,500 flat** for non-managed clients. That's roughly 10 hours — get in, look around, build the report, run the meeting — assuming we have the access we need.
- **Included for fully managed clients** as a value-add (shown as a line item priced and marked "included," not "free," because there's real value). It's a reason for at-risk accounts to renew, and for break-fix / lower-tier clients to move to fully managed.
- The price **qualifies the lead** — a prospect who can't commit to $1,500 isn't ready to spend on AI.
- There's value even in a "no": whether a business adopts AI or not, its competitors are evaluating it, so at a minimum the client learns where they stand in their own market.

**What we do in the assessment:**
1. **Deeper discovery into business problems.** The first conversation surfaced some; here we go deeper. If a business genuinely can't leverage AI, that's a fine answer — we find out here.
2. **Review the environment.** Get access and check whether the infrastructure is ready today and, if not, what it would take (scoped as a separate quote). The recurring reality is years of SharePoint technical debt: garbage in, garbage out — we have to get the data right first.
3. **Evaluate integrations and vendors.** Assess what can actually connect, including calling the client's vendors. AI needs more access than a person does: what someone needs to push buttons is different from what a system needs to pull data and act.
4. **Come back with the verdict + ROI.** What's possible in *their* environment, what it costs to get there (training, setup, ongoing), and a real client example *with the results* — results are the most important part.

**The deliverable is the AI Profit report**, generated from the assessment. It includes:
- Recommended platform and a **foundation-first 90-day roadmap**.
- The estimated **monthly waste** AI can recover (the Profit Gap).
- **Pricing for each path forward**, and a **clear ROI built from the client's own numbers.**

## 04 · What's Included vs. What's Additional

The recurring fee covers **setup, policy, and up to three pre-built integrations.** Everything changed, added, or built after that is additional — that's the line that keeps the fee honest.

**Included in AI Enablement Services (recurring):**
- **Infrastructure setup** — domain and the business/Team account on Claude (our standard platform).
- **AI Acceptable Use Policy** — drafted, deployed, documented (template ready). Introduced in setup, not the first meeting.
- **Up to 3 pre-built integrations** — e.g., Microsoft Exchange, SharePoint, and Teams (that's three on its own), or a CRM — so the AI becomes the central brain tying systems together.
- **Security & governance** — confirm and document that data isn't used for training; corporate (not personal) accounts.
- **User enablement** — AI office hours and a **monthly success-stories email**.
- **Platform & integration-feasibility guidance** — which systems can actually connect.

**Always additional (AI Implementation projects or T&M):**
- Adds, moves & changes after onboarding.
- Custom GPTs, custom skills, agents, workflows, and automations.
- Integrations beyond the included three, or any non-native (MCP / custom) connection — including building a connector *and* a skill that teaches the AI how to use that tool.
- Data cleanup / SharePoint remediation (a prerequisite project when the foundation is messy).
- Large custom app builds (case-by-case, never productized).
- CUI / CMMC-regulated workloads — out of scope at this stage.

**Integration feasibility — three outcomes per system:**
- **Native connector exists** → included (counts toward the three); fast.
- **MCP available** → possible, usually scoped as an additional integration project.
- **No connector / legacy app** → a custom project, or sometimes not worth it — set expectations.

> **The line in the sand:** "We set up and manage the platform — the account, the policy, and up to three native integrations. Anything you want changed, added, or built after that — adds/moves/changes, custom GPTs, skills, workflows, extra integrations — is additional, quoted as a project or billed T&M."

**If a client buys AI direct (not through us):** we don't carry it under enablement. We can support it advisory-only on a premium T&M rate ($250/hr), likely with a liability waiver. Full management is strongly preferred — without visibility into their systems, we can't safely own outcomes. When an unmanaged setup breaks, that's the case for managed services.

## 05 · Delivery Phases

One path, start to finish. Branches are explicit gates, not detours.

| Phase | What happens |
|------|--------------|
| **0 · Temperature check** | Where is the client with AI — resistant, curious, or already using it? Route accordingly. Plant the seed for the resistant and stop there. |
| **1 · AI Profit & Readiness Assessment** | The paid front door. Discovery → environment & integration review → the report (with pricing + ROI). **$1,500 / included for managed.** |
| **2 · Foundation & Data Cleanup** *(conditional)* | Fires only if the assessment says "not ready." Scoped as a project, not recurring. Cost scales with how messy SharePoint / the data is. Don't provision until the foundation passes. |
| **3 · Platform Selection & Provisioning** | Stand up the Claude account, configure the domain, prepare to invite users. Corporate accounts; training off. Connect the up-to-three pre-built integrations. |
| **4 · Governance & Integrations** | Apply the governance baseline, deploy the AUP, wire the connectors. Where useful, build a skill that teaches Claude how to use a connected tool. Clean context in → better output. |
| **5 · Enablement & Adoption** | AI office hours: "try this, come back to me," then level them up. Centralize support through office hours, not ad-hoc calls. |
| **6 · Ongoing Management — the recurring fee** | Light management, adds/moves/changes (billed additional), the monthly success-stories email, and surfacing project opportunities. Token monitoring only where heavy usage exists. |

**Don't build Phase 2+ in detail yet.** The current focus is finalizing the assessment package and what the client gets from it. We work out implementation specifics when a client actually signs up for an assessment — and we can run these conversations off the cuff today.

**Three paths forward — the review-call upsell.** Every assessment ends by positioning **DIY** (the client runs the roadmap), **Consult** (we advise & coach), or **Done-For-You** (we set up and manage — Enablement + Implementation). The report is the upsell trigger; the roadmap is the menu.

**Decision gates (run in order):**
- **Readiness** — do foundations + data pass? *No →* foundation/cleanup project first (Phase 2).
- **Compliance** — any CUI / CMMC data? *Yes →* keep it out of cloud AI; out of scope for now.
- **Procurement** — buying AI through TCT? *No →* T&M / advisory-only with a waiver; full management strongly preferred.
- **Platform** — default Claude; support ChatGPT only if they already use it.
- **Integration** — native connectors for the must-connect systems? *No →* MCP or custom = additional.
- **Expansion** — custom build wanted now? *Yes →* scope as an AI Implementation project with its own maintenance fee.

## 06 · Platform — We Standardize on Claude

**Our standard platform is Claude.** It keeps pulling ahead, and Cowork plus the SharePoint / Microsoft 365 integration are standout capabilities for our target clients. Setting up Claude is part of the enablement package.

- **Don't name the platform to the client in writing.** Part of the assessment is identifying "the proper AI" for their business — we don't tell a client it will always be Claude. This protects us if the landscape shifts.
- **Don't say no to ChatGPT.** If a client already pays for and likes ChatGPT, help them get more out of it. It's not a barrier; it's where they already are.
- **Compliance shifts the math.** A lawyer or dental office (regulated data) changes the platform and architecture calculus — flag it early.

**Cost reality (internal):** operating at team tier runs roughly $200–$350/month per tool. Factor it into the flat fee.

## 07 · Security, Risk & Compliance

Lead from the truth, not from fear. The competitor instinct is "secure the AI," but the headline data-exfiltration fear is largely a false alarm — and saying so honestly is a differentiator.

- **"AI will leak our data to competitors" — false alarm.** Putting company data into an LLM does not let another company query the model for your specifics. The most a model might absorb is generic patterns. Narrow exception: public website content a model cites.
- **Employee offboarding is the real risk.** Work locked in a *personal* AI account walks out the door when the person leaves. **Fix: corporate accounts, not personal.**
- **Provider control — acknowledge, don't over-sell.** You can't control what a provider does with inputs long-term (terms change; platforms get sold). It's the same bargain we've accepted with Google and Microsoft for 20 years. Name it plainly and let the client choose.
- **Cowork — handle with care.** Cowork can control a browser and act for the user. It's *supposed* not to enter passwords, buy things, or delete data, but early cases went off the rails. Before enabling it: include clear liability language / a waiver — "we set these tools up; we are not responsible for what you do with them" — and pilot every deployment.

**Sensitivity & compliance forks:**
- **Hard sensitivity concern** → consider a local / private model; set expectations (fewer integrations, more limits).
- **CUI / CMMC data** → keep it out of cloud AI; out of scope at this stage.
- **Everyone else** → disclaimer, ring-fence training off, corporate accounts, proceed.

> **Open operational issue — admin access & control.** Managing a client's Claude/cloud account needs a designated admin account (billable, roughly $100/month of platform overhead) and carries the risk that a client user changes settings and undoes our work. Because the reseller path isn't available to us right now (see §08), there's no partner-level admin control. This is flagged, not yet solved — resolve it before/at implementation.

## 08 · Pricing & Packaging

**The recurring fee is flat and fixed — not per-user.** The only time we'd use a per-user fee is a rollout to every computer-using employee. Otherwise it's a flat number that reflects our time and the managed environment, and it doesn't change month to month.

**Price is set after the assessment.** Like selling managed services, there's no single price — we get in, learn the systems, then quote. We can give "starting at" ranges and tier examples if a prospect needs a number, and cite a real client's cost-and-result, but the real number follows the assessment. Don't lead with a big minimum; a large number with no context just pushes people away — the assessment is what creates the context.

| Line item | Pricing | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **AI Profit & Readiness Assessment** | **$1,500** (one-time) · **included** for fully managed | The product. ≈10 hrs. May be credited toward setup if they proceed. |
| **AI Enablement — setup** | one-time, flat | Domain + account + AUP + up to 3 native integrations. Number follows the assessment. |
| **AI Enablement — recurring** | flat monthly | Light ongoing management + the monthly success-stories email. Not per-user. |
| **Custom GPT / agent** | from ~$1,000 (by complexity) | AI Implementation. Revisit — may be priced high. |
| **Custom skill** | project quote | A headline value-add (see §09). |
| **Workflow / integration / larger build** | project quote | Can carry its own maintenance fee. |
| **AI consulting (hourly)** | **$250/hr** | Standard rate. |
| **Unmanaged / direct-buy AI support** | $250/hr, advisory-only, waiver required | Full management strongly preferred. |

> Dollar figures other than the $1,500 assessment and the $250/hr rate are working numbers, not finalized list prices. Confirm before quoting live.

**Reseller status.** Our application to resell Claude was declined for now ("come back in six months"). We weren't going to make real margin reselling anyway. Practically: no near-term partner-channel multi-tenancy, billing, or central admin control — we provision and manage on the client's own account, and revisit the partner path in ~6 months.

**Token economics — deliberately de-emphasized.** Most clients won't be metering tokens at all, so this isn't a centerpiece. Operating principles:
- **Recommend tools with flat / effectively unlimited use.** Token costs are unpredictable and may rise — favor predictable, flat-rate seats over metered API.
- **Recommend reversible tools.** Favor solutions where, if the AI stops working, the client simply reverts to the old manual process — they lose the upside, not the business.
- **Bring token planning back only for heavy API / agent work** — that's the one place runaway bills happen, and it's the exception. Don't build a fragile homegrown monitor.

**Margins look strong** because the AI layer rides on the MSP foundation that's already in place. Best value is as an add-on for existing managed clients.

## 09 · AI Implementation Track

Anything custom lives here — never in the recurring fee. Treat it with project discipline so one client can't consume you with live tweak requests.

**Guardrails:**
- Every signature-ready quote states a release cycle (ships at v1.2; new asks queue into 1.3, 1.4).
- No live tweaking — work flows dev → test → production. AI makes this fast, but the guardrails protect your time.
- Communicate the roadmap like a patch list: "here's what's in the next release." It kills the "call you every other day" problem.
- Each build can carry its own monthly maintenance fee — so each one adds to recurring revenue.

**The value-add menu (the easy upsells once a client has a taste):**
- **Custom skills** *(headline item).* "I say a sentence — 'build me X' — it reads the skill and does it." Every integration ultimately leads to a skill, so this belongs front and center.
- **Custom GPTs** — purpose-built assistants (grant-writing for non-profits, proposal drafting, SOP / policy Q&A, onboarding helpers).
- **Claude Cowork enablement** — teach the team to set up and use Cowork (context, skills, scheduled tasks). Requires the liability language and a pilot (see §07).
- **Done-for-you Cowork builds** — recurring scheduled tasks (e.g., "every Monday, pull all invoices, email them to you, and file them in the right SharePoint folder").
- **Data ingestion & routing** — e.g., analyze call logs for missed / unreturned calls and new callers, then auto-ingest and route the data, eliminating manual entry.
- **Workflows & integrations** beyond the included three — native = small, MCP / custom = larger.

**Build the library.** Maintain a catalog of pre-built, quick-win plays we can fire off easily, and **target the roles where the tools deliver most first** — fastest, most visible ROI and your best internal champions. Win loud, then expand.

## 10 · Examples & Proof

**Delivered — call-log automation (Brooms Over Broome).** We analyzed their call logs and found the leak: missed calls, calls never returned, and new callers slipping through. We built automatic ingestion and routing of that call data, eliminating manual entry and making sure no caller falls through the cracks. Recovered real business, zero new headcount — the model ROI story.

**Internal proof — our own AI assistant.** We run a working internal AI assistant that connects to Teams, email, Fathom, and more, and does practical things like filing email attachments into the correct SharePoint folder and notifying the right person. Use it as a live "show, don't tell" demo of what well-connected AI does day to day.

**Archetypes worth re-telling:**
- **Tried it and failed.** A business that went out, tried AI with another vendor, and it didn't work — almost always because they weren't ready and their foundation was a mess. Re-engage with readiness-first: "Here's *why* it didn't work; let's fix the foundation and do it right." They still go through the assessment.
- **The valued, long-tenured client facing hardship.** Do right by them at renewal, and open the AI conversation — the ones who love technology are the best early adopters.
- **The non-technical owner who "got it" instantly** the moment they saw AI do something tangible — clean up a messy SharePoint, control a browser. There are a lot of these people, and they take to it fast.

> **Positioning line that works:** "AI is in its AOL era — mind-blowing and evolving fast. We'll share our journey with you. We could build you the moon today, but you'd pay thousands, and we don't want that. We're getting it to a consumable, easy-to-use stage — and you'll walk away knowing things you had no idea were possible."

## 11 · The Assessment Questions — *for Jim's Review*

> **Jim — this is the open section.** The direction below reflects what we agreed; the exact questions, wording, and order are yours to red-line. Once you're happy, we build the assessment package and define exactly what the client gets. The form is **staff-filled** — we ask these in the meeting; we don't send it to the client (for now).

**Direction:**
1. **Lead with the foundations**, not sales/marketing — systems & integrations, line-of-business apps, Microsoft, SharePoint. We live and breathe this.
2. **Make the profit-zone questions conditional** — ask only the zones tied to a real problem. If they don't have a sales-and-marketing problem, don't ask those questions.
3. **Don't ask about problems we can't solve** — drop or skip categories where we have no solution, so the report never promises what we can't deliver.
4. **Nothing is mandatory** — the interviewer decides what to ask; the more left blank, the more upside left on the table.
5. **Add a custom-question bank + a catch-all** — a reusable bank of custom Q&A to pull from, plus a free-form section so nothing gets lost.
6. **Confirm the recommendation, don't pick a vendor from scratch** — we default to Claude; the platform questions confirm fit and capture whether a client leans one way or wants both.
7. **The report shows pricing for all three paths plus a client-numbers ROI.**

**Proposed order:**
1. Systems & Integrations (foundations) → 2. Readiness & Gates (foundations) → 3. Platform Direction (confirm Claude) → 4. Profit Zones 1–6 (conditional) → 5. Custom-question bank + catch-all.

**Working question set (mark it up):**

*Foundations — Readiness & Gates*
- Microsoft environment set up and healthy? *(Healthy / Partial / Not set up / Unknown)* — foundation gate.
- Where does data live, and is it clean and connectable? *(Clean / Some cleanup / Messy / Unknown)* — data gate.
- Any CUI / CMMC-regulated data? *(No / Some / Yes / Unsure)* — compliance fork.
- Anyone already using AI, and is it governed? *(None / Some, ungoverned / Some, governed / Widespread)* — shadow-AI risk.
- Buying AI through us, or already have / insist on your own? *(Through TCT / Already have it / Insist on own / Undecided)* — scope fork.

*Foundations — Systems & Integrations*
- Primary line-of-business applications?
- Which systems must the AI connect to / pull context from? *(maps to the up-to-3 included integrations)*
- Any custom or legacy apps with no modern API?
- Where does business-critical data actually live? *(M365 / SharePoint / CRM / file shares / a LOB DB)*

*Platform Direction (confirm Claude; capture lean)*
- Main thing you want AI to do? · Who uses it most? · Need custom integrations into LOB systems? · Appetite for automation/agentic work? · How deep is your Microsoft 365 usage? · Any hard data-sensitivity that might need a local/private model?

*Profit Zones (conditional — ask only the zones with a real problem)*
- **Acquisition / Marketing** — content creation, lead capture, outbound, attribution, lead nurture.
- **Conversion / Sales** — speed-to-lead, admin bloat, ghosting follow-up, qualification, call analytics.
- **Fulfillment / Delivery** — first-48-hours onboarding, scaling breakpoint, reporting drag, SOP adherence, margin killers.
- **Retention / Support** — repetitive questions, off-hours support, founder dependency, churn signals, knowledge access.
- **Administration / Finance-HR** — invoicing & collections, scheduling, HR onboarding, data entry, information silos.
- **Strategy / Leadership** — real-time dashboard, time-in-the-weeds, decision data vs. gut, the "$15/hr task," the "vacation test."
- Each zone captures an **estimated monthly waste ($)** that anchors the report's Profit Gap.

*Catch-all* — anything that didn't fit above.

**Open questions for Jim:**
- Which profit zones do we keep, and which do we drop because we don't have a clean solution for them today?
- Any foundation/systems questions missing that you'd always ask?
- What goes in the custom-question bank to start?
- Anything else the report must include beyond pricing + a client-numbers ROI?
